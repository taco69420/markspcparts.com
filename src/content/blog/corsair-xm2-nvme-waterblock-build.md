If you saw the short — welcome. We didn't have enough time in the video to cover the full build, so here's the complete breakdown.

This started with a Corsair XM2 M.2 waterblock I'd been sitting on and a Samsung 980 1TB I had spare. The block was unpowered, meaning it's passive — no pump, no fitting ports of its own, just direct contact cooling designed to bolt onto your drive and integrate into an existing loop or just pull heat into a heatsink-style chassis. The install itself is fast. Here's how it actually went.

---

## Out of the Box

![Corsair XM2 M.2 waterblock — sealed retail packaging on workbench](/images/posts/nvme-waterblock/starting-shot.jpg)

Sealed retail box, never opened. The XM2 comes packaged with everything you need — the top block, the bottom mounting plate, thermal pads, and documentation. First thing out was the manual and the accessory tray sitting on top.

![Corsair XM2 box open — manual and documentation visible on top layer](/images/posts/nvme-waterblock/ssd-out-of-box.jpg)

Under the docs you get the block itself. Two main pieces: the top section which is the actual waterblock body, and the bottom casing that the SSD mounts into before the top clamps down.

---

## First Look at the Block

![Corsair XM2 waterblock top half — first impressions, copper contact plate visible](/images/posts/nvme-waterblock/block-first-impresions.jpg)

The build quality on the XM2 is solid. The contact plate on the underside is copper, which is what actually touches the NAND and controller on the drive. Corsair ships pre-applied thermal pads on it — two pieces, one for the controller and one for the NAND stack.

---

## Thermal Pads Off

![Corsair XM2 waterblock — protective film and thermal pads being removed](/images/posts/nvme-waterblock/thermal-pads-off.jpg)

Before assembly you pull the protective backing off the thermal pads. They're already positioned correctly from the factory, so there's no guesswork on placement. This is the step most people overthink — the pads are pre-cut and pre-positioned, you just peel the film.

---

## SSD Goes In

![Samsung 980 1TB sliding into Corsair XM2 bottom mounting bracket](/images/posts/nvme-waterblock/ssd-slide-in.jpg)

The Samsung 980 1TB slides into the bottom casing at the M.2 angle — same as dropping it into a motherboard slot. The bottom plate holds it at the correct position so the thermal pads land exactly where they need to when the top goes on.

![Samsung 980 1TB seated in Corsair XM2 bottom casing, fully open view](/images/posts/nvme-waterblock/ssd-open.jpg)

![Closer view — Samsung 980 seated and aligned in XM2 mounting bracket](/images/posts/nvme-waterblock/ssd-open-further.jpg)

Drive seated, alignment looks right. The 980's controller and NAND are exposed upward, exactly where the copper pads will make contact.

---

## Top Snaps On

From here it's straightforward — the top block sets down over the bottom casing and snaps into place. No tools needed for the basic assembly. The design is clip-fit, so it's one motion.

![Corsair XM2 fully assembled with Samsung 980 1TB — front view](/images/posts/nvme-waterblock/ssd-compleate.jpg)

![Corsair XM2 fully assembled — rear view showing bottom mounting plate](/images/posts/nvme-waterblock/ssd-compleate-back.jpg)

That's the finished build. Front and back. The whole process took maybe two minutes from opening the box to done. The drive is fully encased — copper contact on the top, the mounting plate on the bottom, thermal pads sandwiched between both and the NAND.

---

## The Point

The XM2 is a passive block. It's designed to mount inside a case that has active water cooling running through the drive bay, or to be used as an oversized heatsink. If you're running a custom loop with an M.2 slot in a waterblock-compatible position this is the direct solution. If you're using it standalone it'll still pull heat better than bare drive or a standard aluminum heatsink, but it needs airflow to do it properly.

Samsung 980 1TB is a solid drive for this treatment — PCIe 3.0, 1TB, runs warm under sustained loads which is exactly the scenario where contact cooling makes a difference.

Total time: two minutes. Total tools required: none.
