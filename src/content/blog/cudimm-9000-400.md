## What These Are

To put this in context: 9000 MT/s CUDIMMs in 2026 are some of the fastest consumer DDR5 money can buy. The CK (Clock Driver) in CUDIMM re-buffers the clock signal on the stick itself, which lets the memory controller push way higher frequencies than it could with a standard UDIMM. It's the same principle as RDIMMs in servers but adapted for consumer platforms.

![Close-up of G.Skill Trident Z5 CK sticks showing the CUDIMM label](/images/posts/cudimm-9000-400/up-close-cudimm.jpg)

A 32GB DDR5 kit of normal speed costs around what I paid for these. I got 48GB at 9000 MT/s.

## Running CUDIMMs on a Z890 Tomahawk

Here's where it gets interesting. The Z890 Tomahawk WiFi is a 4-slot motherboard. It is not officially a CUDIMM-optimized board. Conventional wisdom says you want a board with proper CUDIMM trace routing and validated support to get the most out of these sticks.

I slapped them in anyway.

They run. They run well. Running them at **8800 MT/s** on the Tomahawk — not the full 9000 the kit is rated for, but still absurd for a 2-stick consumer setup. Paired with a Core Ultra 9 285K and I'm seeing **132 GB/s read bandwidth** at the peak — which is getting close to what I was seeing out of the 6-channel Dominus Extreme setup with the W-3175X. On two sticks. On a consumer Z890 board.

Either the 285K has a really strong IMC, the Tomahawk has better traces than people give it credit for, or both. Probably both.
